Anger and Distrust:

North Carolina’s Divided Culture Shown Through the Lens of COVID-19


Davis Barrow

Introduction

North Carolina is separated into three primary regions: the piedmont, coastal plains, and mountains. For the purposes of this study, the coastal plains will be referred to as “eastern NC,” a more commonly-used term. The piedmont, which is the largest region by population, encompasses the metropolitan areas of Raleigh and Charlotte. Eastern NC primarily consists of rural farming towns.

On a drive through Eastern NC, one would pass through miles and miles of open land filled with rows of tobacco, cotton, or other various “cash crops.” Dilapidated tobacco barns and small, tired-looking homes would appear in sparse clusters alongside the occasional barbecue restaurant or shiny new Ford dealership. During state and national elections, this region tends to vote for more conservative candidates.

While there is much open space in the Piedmont, the vast majority of the population is clustered in Charlotte or Raleigh NC. These are midsize cities (comparable in population to Atlanta or Austin 20-30 years ago). This region usually votes for more moderate or liberal national and state candidates.

As the cities of Raleigh and Charlotte have grown, a stronger cultural divide has emerged between the regions of Eastern NC and the Piedmont. “On the street” in Raleigh or Charlotte, it is not uncommon to hear white liberal families discussing their dissatisfaction with the Republican-dominated State Legislature. Many of the districts in Eastern NC are responsible for Davis Barrow this Republican-dominated Legislature, which is responsible for passing controversial bills such as HB-2, commonly known as the “2017 North Carolina Bathroom Bill.”

While the cultural divide between Eastern NC and the Piedmont could be an issue of class discrepancies (cities tend to have more wealth than rural areas), this study seeks to determine what cultural differences, if any, exist between one class of individuals in the Piedmont and Eastern NC. The Piedmont will be represented by results from Raleigh, NC. Eastern NC will be represented by results from small towns including Spring Hope, Wilson, Rocky Mount, New Bern, Goldsboro, and others. All these towns were selected because they are rural, but simultaneously well-populated enough to have numerous big-name retail establishments. Working class, automotive workers were selected as the target group of this study as these individuals fill the following criteria: 1) They are an essential business and will remain open during COVID-19 2) Most of these workers make around the U.S. Median Income 3) People working in the automotive industry are still significantly impacted by the lack of people driving due to COVID-19.

For this study, automotive workers were considered a “community.” This study hoped to avoid comparing political beliefs of a rich city community to a poorer country community. By targeting automotive workers, the voters would all make similar salaries, be surrounded by similar environments (parts, cars, toxic fluids, waste, etc.), and possibly have similar interests, as many individuals choose to work for a mechanic shop, body shop, or auto parts store due to a personal passion for cars.

This study initially planed to demonstrate results through a collage of interviews. However, due to privacy concerns of the interviewed individuals and their employers, this study will instead display results through statements (derived from previous interviews) on which employees voted. Additionally, an anonymous transcript of an interview will be available as an appendix.

16 Various locations were visited and 53 employees were initially interviewed. These locations included the following: Discount Tire, Firestone, O’Reilly’s Auto Parts, Advance Auto Parts, Auto Zone, Napa Auto Parts, Tunstall-Williams Body Shop, Kenny Hawkins Automotive, and Henry’s Muffler Shop. Well into the development of the project, phone calls and/or emails from managers and owners of the aforementioned places of business requested their employees’ interviews be permanently erased and not used in any online publication (which conflicted with the plan for this report). Thus, a new sample of 20 employees were prompted to vote on their feelings about statements derived from the first sample’s interviews.

Analysis:

All data derived from Statements section and Figure #1. Sources included in Sources.

Within the community of automotive workers, there were substantial amounts of overlap in behavior, even in light of potential differences in political opinions/beliefs about the virus. On first glance, it is apparent that hardly any respondents took the option to “decline” a question. Except for four “decline” votes by Eastern NC employees in response to the statement about social distancing being effective, every employee chose to answer every question. This sheds insight into how passionate many of the members of the automotive community are about politics. The willingness with which these voters responded to the statements reveals how eager these workers were to convey their opinions.

Figure #1 demonstrates how voters in Eastern NC tended to have more diversity of opinions than voters in the Piedmont. The Piedmont voters would typically submit similar opinions while the Eastern NC voters were more equivocal. For example in the statements about racism and blaming China for this situation, the Eastern NC voters answered a broad spectrum of agreements and disagreements, while Piedmont voters tended to vote similarly condemning racism or blame.

On the topic of questioning authority, Piedmont voters were much more likely to believe what they had been told than Eastern NC voters. For example, 2/3 of Eastern NC voters agreed with the statement about the Coronavirus being a “liberal hoax,” while every Piedmont voter disagreed with this statement. This sharp difference in responses cannot simply be attributed to political leanings/views—there are almost certainly Republicans and Democrats in each pool.

The Piedmont voters were more likely to determine that statewide suffering for the common good is beneficial to individuals than Eastern NC voters. All the Eastern NC voters suggested the statewide restriction was not beneficial to themselves, while 3/5 of Piedmont voters suggested they were beneficial to themselves.

While the interviews would have been helpful for better understanding the moods and feelings of the various employees, the fixed statements allowed the study to draw clearer conclusions about peoples’ thoughts. Instead of an infinitely broad spectrum of thought, the statements stratified the results in a very efficient manner.

This study demonstrates how even across one community of automotive workers, core beliefs about the good of society vs. the good of the individual, the role of governmental authority, and the prevalence of diverse opinions can vary extremely broadly. At the same time, the underlying passion to share their views was present in almost every worker. This study suggests there may be some underlying cultural differences between Eastern NC and the Piedmont. Future studies could narrow down/analyze the source of these varying cultures. Eastern NC is home to numerous military bases and countless farms, while the Piedmont houses high-tech companies and universities. While this study offers some conclusions about the cultural differences between Eastern NC and the Piedmont, it further enriches the questions of how we stratify/outline communities. Some future questions to be explored include, “Must communities share the same culture?” and “How do cultural divides affect our communities?”
