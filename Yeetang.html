COVID-19: Community of Maps, Data, and the People Behind Them

Yeetang Kwok

At the beginning of spring break, our family travelled to Florida, unknowing of the drastic changes that would take place during our stay. As cases started rising in Massachusetts, Toronto, and nearby Miami, we were worried and confused about where to go. Data and maps have been a clear and concise way for our family and many others to scope out the severity of the crisis at the local and national levels. In fact, the narrative of coronavirus has been driven by the numbers, whether it be the number of positive cases, the number of deaths, etc. Turning onto the news channels, here are some of the figures that we are shown.

The first questions that I asked myself are: what are some of the key figures in this crisis, and what do they mean? For example, something that sounds as simple as the death rate can be very different across states, and even counties. Some hospitals count coronavirus deaths as those directly caused by the disease. Other hospitals include deaths caused by other underlying conditions but accelerated by coronavirus. Then, there is also the question of data visualization and modelling. How are we to interpret these models? Why are there stark disagreements within the epidemiologist community?. In addition to approaching this crisis from a “numbers” perspective, I will be documenting the community of scientists, epidemiologists, and statisticians who spent countless hours compiling and making sense of the data.

I.   Tracking Data and Models

The COVID Tracking Project
By Robinson Meyer, Alexis Madrigal, and Jeff Hammerbacher

Background

The COVID Tracking Project is a volunteer operation launched by The Atlantic in response to the initial lack of comprehensive federal datasets. At the onset of the outbreak, the CDC has only published the case numbers, which only gives an incomplete picture without hospitalization rates and positive rates. The project grew from a team of three to more than hundreds of volunteers from many different professions.

Features

Data Tracking: The Covid Tracking Project assembles data directly from state/district/territory public health authorities. They receive updates from website-scrapers but enter the data manually to ensure accuracy. The national dataset displays testing results and deaths while the state dataset displays test results, hospitalizations, ICU and ventilator usage, as well as recovered numbers when available. Justifications for Data: The Covid Tracking Project provides clear explanations for inclusions and omissions. For example, the reason for the exclusion of hospitalization numbers in national data is due to inconsistent collection methods and definitions in different states. For state numbers, there are notes elucidating testing situations. Example below for testing notes in Massachusetts.

“Positives include results from the CDC. Total tested includes repeat testing on individuals. State lab numbers are as of 8 am, other lab numbers are as of 11:30 am. Commercial lab numbers are subject to change. For 4/20, Cumulative Hospitalized, Cumulative in ICU includes both confirmed and suspected cases.”

Data Grade: Each state is given a grade for the comprehensiveness of their reported data. The score is determined on these four factors:

+1 for reporting positives reliably
+1 for reporting negatives sometimes
+1 for reporting negatives reliably
+1 for reporting all commercial tests
A score of 1 corresponds to D, 2 to C, 3 to B, and 4 to A.

COVID Racial Data Tracker: Partnering with Ibram Kendi and the Antiracist Research & Policy Center at American University, the team will launch a racial data tracker that delineates COVID-19 deaths by race.

The Johns Hopkins Dashboard
By Lauren Gardner and Ensheng Dong

Background

You’ve probably seen this dashboard at some point. The Dashboard was created by Lauren Gardner and Ensheng Dong at Johns Hopkins Center for Systems Science and Engineering.While their intended audience was for the academic world, their dataset has become one of the most authoritative and receives over 1 billion requests a day. What started as a team of 3 grew to nearly two dozen working around the clock. It has displayed a good overview of global and local progression of the outbreak.

Features

Data-tracking: Updated automatically using semi-automated living data stream strategy and historically reported higher values than WHO because. Data garnered from national agencies such as the Chinese CDC, Government of Canada, CDC etc.

Display: The main dashboard displays cases by region, deaths by region, and an interactive map and timeline. It shows cumulative national cases in every country except the United States (county-wide), Canada (province-wide), China (province-wide), and Australia (state-wide). The dashboard uses the Arcgis interactive mapping software.

Source Bar: Lists main data sources from the WHO to social media handles. Includes a Lancet Infectious disease letter explaining data retrieval process. Clarifies key terminology such as death totals being both presumptive and confirmed deaths.

Trends and Insights

What is the mortality rate of coronavirus, and what does it mean?

The mortality rate differs vastly across different nations because of different methods of counting and situations. For example, in Germany and Spain, the death toll only accounts for hospital deaths. Whereas in Belgium, all presumed coronavirus deaths in hospitals, care homes, and other locations are counted, leading to a high mortality rate.

Other factors for this difference in case-fatality ratio (deaths per 100 cases) include testing availability, hospital capacity, and demographics of a nation. We could see the mortality rate drop drastically as testing becomes more widespread among asymptomatic or mildly-symptomatic people.

The University of Washington Institute for Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) Model

Background

The University of Washington IHME Model is one of the most frequently cited coronavirus models. It has played a large role in government action in the United States. Significant events such as the initial stay-at-home order and the “10000 deaths” estimate all came from the IMHE model.
